The Story of Levi LeMaine

Levi LeMaine was born to Loretta and Logan LeMaine in the coal-mining town of Cattlerun, New Catalonia in the year 961 By all accounts his youth was unremarkable, growing up on a ranch outside of town he managed to largely avoid the coal-mining fever that swept up many of the other youths in the burgeoning frontier village. Rough, charming, handsome, and an all-around fine boy; that’s what the folks from the village would call him as he matured in a strapping young man, the charm of his dark red complexion making him the fancy of more than a few of the young women around his home. He’d taken up his mother’s hobby of skeet shooting to develop his skills with both a long-barreled rifle and the revolver that he carried at his side for settling disputes. From the time he could call himself a teenager he’d already taken remarkably well to the life a bona fide cowboy—cards, gambling, horses, and cattle ranching left everyone thinking that there was no question, he would be the one to take over the family farm and continue the LeMaine ranch on to the next generation. Unfortunately, he would never get the chance.

Levi died, on the night of the 13th of Midyear, 985. While bringing the cattle home from grazing a few hours from home, something startled the cattle and sent them running. With him and his horse caught in the middle of a stampede he tried desperately to calm the herd—but to no avail. Levi died, his broken body thrown from his horse and trampled, alone and farm from home in the radiant desert sunset. But his time on Aarde was not yet up, as fate would have it. His soul was snatched from its ascent by a whimsical being, a wanderer of the wastes, an entity of great power that offered Levi a deal. His life, for his luck. Levi thought for a long time on if a deal with such a being was wise, but ultimately decided that he was not ready for the great beyond yet—so a deal was struck. Levi gave to this being his luck, the thing he had so depended on in his card games and shooting championships. The creature reached out and plucked a radiant droplet of dew from his core—and threw him with some force back to the barren earth on which he laid.

As he drew in his first breath, he felt as though a piece of him was missing. Such a deal may have been a mistake. As he stood examining his repaired body, he turned quickly to see a small pack of coyotes approaching him—thinking him a meal—and drew his revolver only to hear a dull click. His revolver had jammed. When he made it home, he was covered in claw- marks and bites, but carried with him two fresh coyote pelts. His family questioned him extensively on what had happened, but he assured them he had simply fallen from his horse and had to walk back. He resolved that telling people would only bring unnecessary complications.

He would quickly discover that his lack of luck would mean more than never winning another hand of cards or game of dice in the saloon in town. He found himself down on his luck in every way: when he tried to work his cattle would disappear the moment he turned his head— needing a search party to track them down, when he tried to make a little money through shooting, his gun would jam at critical moments, or the wind would shift the targets at the last moment. When he was finally forced to resort to the bank for a loan to keep his family afloat, lady luck betrayed him again and cast him into destitution. After some deliberation he finally decided that he would leave the ranch in the hands of his father and capable younger brother. They could handle it, at least enough to keep it afloat if he could only get his rotten luck out of the way. So, he made certain that the bank knew it was him and him alone who took that loan, grabbed his hat, gun and best pair of boots, saddled his horse, and rode out of town.

He found himself having to resort to the occasional bounty hunting to get by. A dangerous business to be sure, and even worse if one was plagued by a lack of luck, but his steady aim, wits, and fast-talking charm got him through it with just a few bumps and bruises. All the while he stayed on the run from the law, the bank ever-intent on getting back the money they’d loaned him to keep his family’s farm afloat.

Last night he found himself desperately in need of a new saddle but hadn’t the money to afford it. In a last-ditch effort he decided to rig a game of dice at the saloon in the town he was spending the night. It went perfectly, a perfect switch out to his liar’s dice and he played it even and cool the entire time only to find… his own cheating dice betrayed him. He lost his horse in that bet, leaving him a cowboy without cows, a horse, or even a hat—he’d bet that too. The only thing left to him: the boots on his feet and the gun on his hip that his mother had given him near- on a decade ago, that was something he’d never give up, not while he still drew breath.
